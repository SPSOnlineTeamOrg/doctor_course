Breakdown (AChE) and recycling

The concentration of neurotransmitter within the synapse is tightly regulated to ensure appropriate stimulation of the post-synaptic receptors. In cholinergic synapses this is achieved by the enzyme acetylcholinesterase (AChE) which is bound to the basement membrane within the synaptic cleft. A second type of cholinesterase, butyrylcholinesterase, is widely distributed throughout the body, and also exists in a soluble form in plasma.

After receptor activation, ACh is released back into the synaptic cleft where it is free to bind to AChE. Once bound, the ester bond in ACh is hydrolysed releasing choline (which is reused) and acetic acid (which is excreted). Drugs which block AChE activity (anticholinesterases such as physostigmine) prolong the half-life of ACh in the synapse resulting in enhanced transmission at cholinergic synapses.

Choline produced from ACh hydrolysis is recycled through the high-affinity choline uptake system present on the pre-synaptic terminal for use in the synthesis of ACh. It is the uptake of choline that is the rate-limiting step in the synthesis of ACh.
