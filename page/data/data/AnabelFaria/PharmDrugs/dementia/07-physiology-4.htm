ACh receptors

Both nicotinic and muscarinic receptors are present at cholinergic synapses in the central nervous system, and both subtypes are present at most synapses.

Nicotinic receptors are encoded by several gene families (α1-α10; β1-β4; ε, δ, γ). Although most of these subunits can be detected in the CNS, most nicotinic receptors consist of either the α7 subunit alone, or a mixture of the α4 and β2 subunits (α4β2). All nicotinic receptors are ligand-gated ion channels, which open upon binding of ACh resulting in an increased flux of calcium and sodium into the neuron. The predominant role of nicotinic receptors in the CNS is to modulate the release of neurotransmitters (including that of ACh itself), and as such most CNS nicotinic receptors are located on presynaptic terminals. Therefore, binding of ACh to these receptors will increase calcium entry into the presynaptic terminal resulting in elevated release of neurotransmitter.
