In addition to the increased degeneration of cholinergic pathways, a significant decrease in the function of surviving cholinergic neurons has been reported in AD patients, with most of these deficits being linked to the presence of Aβ peptide in the neuritic plaques. ACh synthesis is significantly reduced through a combination of factors.

Firstly, the choline uptake through the high-affinity transporter is reduced, as is pyruvate dehydrogenase activity (one of the enzymes that synthesises acetyl-CoA), and there is a reduction in ChAT activity is also found in those with AD. Taken together, these changes significantly reduce the capacity of neurons to synthesise ACh.

Secondly, ACh release is reduced in the presence of Aβ through an action on the α7-subtype of nicotinic receptor. Aβ-mediated antagonism of α7 receptors attenuates calcium entry into the presynaptic terminal, thus reducing the signal for vesicle fusion and subsequent ACh release.

Simultaneously, there is an attenuation signalling through the post-synaptic M1 muscarinic as GTPase activity and IP3 production are reduced. In summary, the ability of surviving cholinergic neurons to function effectively is compromised in Alzheimer’s Disease through a combination of reduced synthesis, less effective release and reduced efficacy of post-synaptic signalling. It is because of this, that anticholinesterases, which prolong the action of ACh, have been used as drugs in the treatment of Alzheimer’s Disease patients.
