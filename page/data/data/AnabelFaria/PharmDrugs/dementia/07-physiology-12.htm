Cholinesterase Inhibitors

Acetylcholine (ACh) that is released from cholinergic neurons is broken down extremely rapidly by the enzyme acetylcholinesterase (AChE). AChE works extremely rapidly, with up to 10000 molecules of ACh hydrolysed by each AChE active site per second, meaning that the half-life of ACh is very short. Cholinesterase inhibitors (ChEIs) bind to AChE, preventing the hydrolysis of ACh, and consequently increasing the time that ACh is available to activate the receptors. Several ChEIs are used clinically in conditions to potentiate cholinergic transmission – including pyridostigmine and neostigmine for myasthenia gravis and physostigmine for the treatment of glaucoma.

Three ChEIs have been licensed for use in AD patients – Donepezil, Galantamine and Rivastigmine. These were initially licensed for use in patients with a confirmed diagnosis of mild to moderate AD (see National Institute for Clinical Excellence (NICE) guidelines). These guidelines have recently been reviewed and new recommendations (currently in draft form) suggest that ChEIs should only be used in patients with confirmed moderate AD (see the NICE review). However, this decision has raised a number of concerns amongst health professionals (for example see here), and a final decision has yet to be taken.

A number of clinical trials of all three drugs have suggested clinical efficacy in many, but not all, patients and as such regular review of cognitive state is required for continuation of treatment. As with other ChEIs, the principle side effects reported for donepezil, rivastigmine and galantamine are gastrointestinal tract disturbances (nausea, vomiting and diarrhoea) and cardiovascular (bradycardia and blood pressure disturbances).
