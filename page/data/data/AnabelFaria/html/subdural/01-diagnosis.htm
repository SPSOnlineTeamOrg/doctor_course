2.1 Diagnosis of subdural haematoma
Help References

Diagnosis of subdural haematoma depends upon the clinical history, clinical examination radiological (CT and MRI) findings.

Subdural haematomas may be acute, in which case the patient develops signs (often becoming drowsy and loses consciousness) within minutes or a few hours of the head injury. In chronic subdural haematomas, there is a history of more insidious onset of clinical signs of raised intracranial pressure.

In older people, the head injury may be very slight and may almost go unnoticed. Clinical signs may not manifest themselves for several weeks, when the patient becomes disinterested, confused and drowsy. Eventually, the patient may lapse into coma.

Examination of the patient may reveal the site of the head injury and more general signs such as deteriorating level of consciousness. The raised intracranial pressure may cause papilloedema that can be seen on examination of the fundi, and dilatation and lack of reactivity to light of the pupil on the same side as the haematoma. This is discussed in more detail in the Complications section (Sections 2.5).
