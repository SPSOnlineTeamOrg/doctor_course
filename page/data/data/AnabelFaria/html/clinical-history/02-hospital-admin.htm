Question

A space occupying lesion inside the skull (such as haemorrhage or tumour) often results in raised intracranial pressure. How do you think that pressure rises?

Select the image which most closely resembles your answer.

a. in a linear fashion as a straight line with time

b. exponentially

c. not at all
Sorry, that is not right. The pressure rises exponentially. Initially, with the evacuation of CSF and blood from inside the skull, there is no rise in pressure despite the rise in volume of the space occupying lesion, in this case a subdural haematoma. At a critical point, the skull cannot accommodate further rise in volume and the pressure rises steeply. Symptoms such as drowsiness and unconsciousness occur at (a) in the lower part of the ascending pressure curve and death ensues as the pressure rises steeply at point (b).
