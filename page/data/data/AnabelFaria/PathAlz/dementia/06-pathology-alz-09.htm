Neurofibrillary tangles were first identified in neurons by silver staining techniques and were subsequently shown to be composed of tau and other proteins attached to ubiquitin. Tau is a microtubule-associated protein concerned with axoplasmic flow. In Alzheimer’s Disease, tau accumulates in a hyperphosphorylated form in association with the protein ubiquitin. Damaged proteins are normally removed from the cytoplasm by attachment to ubiquitin leading to enzymatic degradation by proteasomes (see the section on Age changes of the brain). It is unclear why proteasomal degradation of hyperphosphorylated tau is defective in Alzheimer’s Disease.

Amyloid beta (Aβ) is derived by a proteolytic cleavage of a 700 amino acid transmembrane protein, amyloid precursor protein (APP), encoded by a gene on chromosome 21.
