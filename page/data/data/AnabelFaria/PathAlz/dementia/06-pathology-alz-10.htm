Sorry, that's not right. The transmembrane amyloid precursor protein (APP) is cleaved by a series of secretases to form Aβ peptides of varying length but mainly 40 or 42 amino acids long (Aβ 1-42 is less soluble than Aβ 1-40). Mutations in the APP gene (listed as a series of names) interfere with the cleavage and affect the structure of Aβ often with the production of more Aβ 1-42 than normal.

APP appears to play a role in cell communication. In common with other chemically different forms of amyloid, Aβ tends to form insoluble fibrils with a β-pleated sheet structure. When Aβ in its soluble form is released from cells it is normally removed from the brain by a number of mechanisms that seem to fail in the elderly and in patients with Alzheimer’s Disease. Aβ may pass directly back into the blood through receptor mediated mechanisms or it is degraded in the brain parenchyma by enzymes such as Neprilysin.
