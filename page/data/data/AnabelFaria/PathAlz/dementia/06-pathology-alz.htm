4.6c Pathology: Alzheimer's Disease 1/12

As first described by Alzheimer in 1907, Alzheimer’s Disease is characterised pathologically by a variable degree of cerebral atrophy and histologically by the presence of neurofibrillary tangles (accumulation of tau protein and ubiquitin) within neurons and the presence of amyloid beta (Aβ) in the extracellular spaces of the brain (Senile or Neuritic plaques).

Compare the two images above. Both show the left lateral surface of the cerebral hemispheres and the cerebellum (bottom right).

In the brain on the left, the gyri are wide and the sulci are very narrow; blood vessels are seen coursing over the surface of the cerebral hemispheres. No abnormality is evident.

However, the brain on the right is from a patient who died with severe dementia and by comparison it shows narrowing of the gyri and widening of the sulci, particularly in the frontal temporal regions (left side of picture) but less so in the occipital region (right side of picture). Such cerebral atrophy may be identified by CT or MRI scans.
