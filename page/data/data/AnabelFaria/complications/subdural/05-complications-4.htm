A more significant site of herniation, however, is through the tentorium cerebelli. The medial aspect of the temporal lobe is forced through the opening in the tentorium cerebelli between the mid-brain and the edge of the tentorium itself. Such displacement of the medial part of the temporal lobe (the uncus and parahippocampal gyrus) through the tentorial opening has two major effects:

  1. Compression of the third cranial nerve (oculomotor nerve) results in dilatation of the pupil in the ipsilateral eye (often referred to as a "blown" pupil).
  2. Compression of branches of the posterior cerebral artery as they cross the surface of the parahippocampal gyrus may result in infarction of the occipital cortex (including the visual cortex). Patients who recover from this injury may be left with visual defects. In more severe cases the under surface of the temporal lobe is infarcted.

More life-threatening is compression of the mid-brain; veins on the surface of the mid-brain are compressed resulting in venous infarction and haemorrhage into the centre of the mid-brain.

In some cases, displacement of the mid-brain due to a subdural haematoma on one side results in bruising of the cerebral peduncle on the contralateral side. Paradoxically, this injury to the corticospinal tracts in the cerebral peduncle results in hemiparesis on the right; that is the same side as the haematoma causing the displacement.
