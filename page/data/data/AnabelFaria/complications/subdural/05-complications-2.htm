The figure below shows how the haematoma on the right has displaced the cerebral hemisphere in the direction of the black arrows. The parahippocampal gyrus on the medial aspect of the temporal lobe (**) has herniated through the tentorium cerebelli. This has compressed the midbrain and caused haemorrhage in the centre of the midbrain. Herniation at this site will also compress the third cranial nerve (oculomotor nerve) causing the pupil to dilate. Displacement of the brain has also resulted in herniation of the cingulated gyrus under the falx cerebri (*) and distortion of the lateral ventricles.

The falx cerebri is in the median plane and separates the two cerebral hemispheres. A horizontal sheet of dura, the tentorium cerebelli, separates the middle and anterior cranial fossae containing the cerebral hemispheres from the posterior fossa containing the brain stem and cerebellum. The relatively small opening in the tentorium cerebelli allows the mid-brain to pass through from the posterior fossa into the middle cranial fossa. As a subdural haematoma enlarges, it displaces the cerebral hemisphere in a medial direction and this forces the medial part of the frontal lobe (cingulated gyrus) under the falx cerebri.
