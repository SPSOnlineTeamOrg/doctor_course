There are several types of intracranial haemorrhage which are listed below. Click on each of these in turn to find out more about them.

Intracerebral haemorrhage

Intracerebral haemorrhage, in which there is bleeding into the brain tissue itself, occurs mainly in patients with hypertension, although intracerebral haemorrhage may occur due to trauma, clotting disorders and occasionally due to haemorrhage into tumours in the brain. Haemorrhage associated with Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (See the section on Pathogenesis of Alzheimer’s disease) is one of the commonest causes of intracerebral haemorrhage in the over 75 age group.

Subarachnoid haemorrhage

Subarachnoid haemorrhage is mainly due to the rupture of saccular aneurysms or arteriovenous malformations or due to trauma; blood spreads throughout the subarachnoid space and may cause vasospasm of cerebral arteries resulting in cerebral infarction.

Extradural haematoma

Extradural haematoma, usually involves the middle meningeal artery or its branches following a skull fracture, occurs mainly in young adults in whom the dura is easily separated from the skull. Extradural haematomas are often associated with contusion of the underlying brain.

Clinically, loss of consciousness may be delayed for some hours with extradural haematoma, the so-called 'lucid interval'. It is thought that the extradural haematoma forms soon after the head injury and that loss of consciousness occurs as the contused underlying brain swells in the hours after the head injury; this results in raised intracranial pressure and decreasing conscious levels.

Subdural haematoma

Subdural haematoma may occur at any age and may be the result of severe trauma such as a road traffic accident or from minor injury especially in the elderly. The two main causes of subdural haematoma are:
  1. Leakage of blood from severe contusion or bruising on the surface of the cerebral hemisphere related to either the direct impact of head injury or due to a contre-coup injury on the other side of the brain.
  2. The acceleration and deceleration in the skull and the brain (often following falls and assaults) are thought to result in tearing of the bridging veins passing from the surface of the cerebral hemisphere to the superior sagittal sinus. Blood leaks from these tears into the subdural compartment.
