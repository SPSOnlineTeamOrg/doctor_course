Subdural haematomas may be acute, with rapid accumulation of blood in the subdural space and loss of consciousness due to raised intracranial pressure, or they may be chronic and develop over 2-3 weeks.

In acute subdural haematomas, the blood clot is usually evacuated by a craniotomy.

In chronic subdural haematomas, when the blood has been in the subdural space for 2 or more weeks, macrophages and fibroblasts invade the surfaces of the haematoma. After several months or years, a tough subdural membrane forms in which the remains of the blood clot are enclosed. In this case, removal of the membrane entails stripping it from the dura at craniotomy.
